## Utilizing plasma cell-free DNA fragmentation hotspots to characterize epigenetic gene expression and regulation of inaccessible human tissues.

Epigenetic mechanisms play a critical role in human disease progression. Some human tissues, like tumors and transplanted organs, are relatively inaccessible; their epigenetics are difficult to monitor non-invasively. One promising approach to understanding the epigenetic influence of these tissues is plasma cell-free DNA (cfDNA) fragmentomics. Circulating cell-free DNA are fragments of DNA released into the peripheral blood, mainly from the apoptosis of diseased living tissues. With developments in sequencing technologies, measuring the fragmentation patterns of cfDNA in a high-throughput and precise manner is extremely accessible. Circulating cell-free DNA from diseased tissues can then be identified and separated by the presence of mutations or single-nucleotide polymorphisms.

Studies have shown a robust correlation between cfDNA fragmentation patterns and the position of gene regulatory components; the presence of a nucleosome at a certain region in the genome reduces cfDNA fragmentation at that region. Therefore, cfDNA fragmentation hotspots can be used to derive the *in vivo* mechanisms of gene regulation in humans. To that end, a computational approach called cell-free DNA fragmentation (CRAG) was developed. Using CRAG, it was demonstrated that cfDNA fragmentation patterns had hotspot abnormalities in multiple early-stage cancers, indicating the potential to utilize fragmentation hotspots in the detection of cancers.
